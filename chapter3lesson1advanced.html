Lesson 3.1 - Forces and Interactions

Chapter 3 is all about forces and their properties. A force is any push or pull that changes an objects velocity. Newton's 1st law states that an object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force. This means that an object must be acted upon by an external force to accelerate. That's essentially what a force is, an interaction with an object that causes it to accelerate. This is defined in Newton's 2nd law, F = ma, where force is defined by an acceleration. There are six common types of forces we work with in this chapter:

  1. Weight
  2. Normal Force
  3. Tension
  4. Friction
  5. Applied
  6. Air Resistance

Weight

Weight is the force of gravity on a given object on it's planet. Mass does not change from planet to planet, but weight does, because it is defined by the force of gravity. Specifically, Newton's 2nd law F = ma can be used to calculate weight, because weight is a force. the acceleration of gravity on Earth is g, and the force in this case is weight, thus yields the equation W = mg. On the moon the acceleration due to gravity is 1/6 of what it is on Earth, thus you weigh 1/6 as much. This is an important distinction between weight and mass, mass is an amount of matter, whereas weight is a force.

Normal Force

So, weight pulls you down. In theory, forces create a change in velocity by pushing or pulling them. So, your velocity should start to point towards the Earth right? And it would, if it weren't for normal force. Newton's 3rd law states that every action has an equal and opposite reaction. This means that for a given gravity vector G that is perpendicular to the surface, there exists a normal force vector N such that |N| = |G| when |V| denotes the magnitude of a vector V. Normal forces run perpendicular to the surface and opposite the gravity vector that points perpendicular to the surface.

Lesson 3.1 - Forces and Interactions

The main idea behind Chapter 3 is the notion of forces. Forces, on a most basic level, is simply a push or pull. If an object is exerting force on another object, we say that they are interacting. There are six types of forces that we cover in this chapter:

  1. Weight
  2. Normal Force
  3. Tension
  4. Friction
  5. Applied
  6. Air Resistance

Weight

Weight refers to the pull of gravity on an object. Weight changes based on what planet you're on, because gravity could be stronger or weaker. Consider this: If gravity is pulling you towards the ground, why don't you fall straight to the center of the Earth? You are exerting some force on the ground, some push, but you're not moving up or down, because the ground actually pushes back. You may have heard before that "every action has an equal and opposite reaction". All this means is that when you push on the ground, it pushes back just as hard, so the pushes cancel and you don't move up or down.

Normal Force

The next type of force is called a normal force. And no, normal in this case does not mean its standard or ordinary. A normal force is a force that is perpendicular to the ground or surface the object is on. Normal force is the force we talked about earlier that pushes back on you. Weight pushes you down, and normal force pushes you up just as strong.

In the diagram above, the horribly drawn brown arrow that points downward is the weight (also known as gravitational force), and the blue arrow represents normal force. The two forces are represented by vectors, and the magnitudes are equal. If the magnitudes of the forces were not the same, then the object would float up or fall down.

Tension

Another type of force is called tension. The easiest way to understand tension is to think of a rope. You could pull a heavy weight with a rope attached to it, but you cant use a rope to push an object. you can take a metal rod attached to something and push or pull it, but with a rope, if you try to push it goes loose, so you can only pull. Tension is a pulling force.

Friction

Friction is one of the more complicated and technical types of forces. For now, we are going to say that friction points opposite of the ground and direction of motion. Friction generally slows down an object and causes it to eventually come to a stop. If you give an object a hard push on the ground and le it slide, eventually it will stop, because of friction. Had there been no friction (and a couple other things) the object would keep sliding forever.

Applied Force

Applied force is probably the first thing you think of when someone mentions the word force. An applied force is basically when a person or object applies a force on another object. Anything such as a person pushing a desk, a machine pushing a car, or otherwise, is considered an applied force.

Air Resistance

The final common type of force is air resistance. Air resistance slows down an object that is moving through the air. This property is not limited to air, the same is true for all fluids. Think about when you try to throw something in water. It probably moves a lot slower than it does through air, and that is because of this resistance. Air resistance specifically deals with the slowing of objects through the air. This force always runs in the opposite direction of the objects motion, similar to friction.

3.1 Example Problems

1. Which types of forces are acting on the red object?? (There are 3 main ones, technically 4)

2. Which types of forces are acting on this person? (There are 2)

3. Which types of forces are acting on this desk? (There are 4 main ones, technically 5)

Answers

1. The main forces acting on the red block are gravity (weight), a normal force, and friction. Gravity points straight down as always, friction goes in the opposite direction the block would be moving, and normal force goes perpendicular to the surface. Technically in the real world this object would be moving through the air and experience air resistance, but it's very small and this problem can be assumed to be done in a vacuum and ignore air resistance. There is no person or other object that is physically pushing the object, so there is no applied force. There are no ropes or strings involved, and so there is also no tension.

2. The two forces acting on this person are gravity and air resistance. Gravity pushes downwards, and air resistance pushes upwards against gravity. While there are strings on the parachute, that tension only affects the parachute, and not the person. The person is also not making any direct contact with any solid surface below him, so there is no friction or normal force. Once again no one is pushing or pulling on him, so there is also no applied force.

3. The main forces are applied force, gravity, normal force, and friction. Once again air resistance could be considered as a technicality. Keep in mind air resistance is used almost exclusively for falling objects, 99% of the time. The applied force is the person pushing on the desk, and as usual gravity is pulling down on it. The desk pushes back off the ground against gravity, which is the normal force. Finally the desk experiences friction because it is being slid across the ground. Had the person picked it up and carried it, there would have been no friction, but the act of sliding creates friction.

Force Diagrams

As you saw in the answers above, we use vectors to model forces. The above force diagrams are technically inaccurate, as the point of them is only to show direction. Vectors also have magnitude, and traditionally we draw longer arrows for vectors with larger magnitudes.

In the diagram above, the vectors are the same length so we say the forces are balanced. This means that the forces cancel each other out and the object does not accelerate at all, it moves at a constant speed.

In this diagram above, the applied force is stronger than the normal force, so the object will accelerate in the direction of the force. In this case the forces are unbalanced. Whichever way the larger arrow points is the direction the object accelerates. A common misconception is that the force arrows describe which way the object is moving. This is not true, the object could still be moving to the right, but it would be accelerating towards the left, or slowing down.

Balanced Force vs Unbalanced Forces Concept Builder

Example 1: Unbalanced Forces - Force Diagrams

Notice that in the direction it states that the object is moving rightward, that's important. Now, we know that forces point in the same direction as acceleration. The two vertical force vectors are going complete opposite directions, so they cancel out. We are left with a force pushing left on an object that is moving right. Thus, the acceleration points left and the object must be slowing down. The forces are unbalanced because the object is acclerating. The forces are only balanced if they all cancel and the object is moving at a constant speed.

Example 2: Balanced Forces - Force Diagrams

In this case, we can see that all of the forces have the same magnitude, so they all cancel out. This means that the object proceeds with a constant speed, and the forces are balanced.

Example 3: Balanced Forces - Position-Time Graphs

In this example, we can see that the position-time graph is linear. This means that the object is moving at a constant speed, and thus the forces are balanced.

Example 4: Unbalanced Forces - Position-Time Graphs

In this case we have to really think and intepret this position-time graph. What is this object doing? well the position is going down so we can say for sure that the object is moving left, and you can see that its beginning to get steeper and steeper, and thus faster and faster. This object is speeding up in the left direction, and therefore has unbalanced forces.

Example 5: Balanced Forces - Motion Diagrams

These are pretty simple. The dots are all evenly spaced, so the object must have a constant speed and therefore balanced forces.

Example 6: Unbalanced Forces - Motion Diagrams

In this case, we see that the object is speeding up because the dots are spreading out in the direction of the motion. Therefore, the forces are unbalanced.

Example 7: Balanced Forces - Velocity-Time Graphs

As we can see, the velocity of this graph is not changing. At every time, the velocity is not going up or down. This means that the object has constant speed, and of course therefore balanced forces.

Example 8: Unbalanced Forces - Velocity-Time Graphs

In this example velicty is decreasing because the line is going down. This means that the object is slowing down and then of course unbalanced because it is accelerating.

Example 9: Unbalanced Forces - Velocity-Time Charts

In these charts, you have to look at the differences in the velocities. From 0 to 1 second the velocity goes from 0 to -2, meaning it decreased by 2. In the next second it decreased by 2 again, etc. This means that the object is speeding up in the negative direction.

Example 11: Balanced Forces - Position-Time Charts

In this example, the position is increasing by 10 every second. This means that it is linear, because the rise over the run is always the same. Therefore the speed is constant, and the force is balanced.
